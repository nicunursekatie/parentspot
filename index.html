Your First Week in the NICU

The Only Three Things That Matter Today:

  • 1. Your baby is in expert hands
  • 2. You're allowed to feel however you feel
  • 3. Taking care of yourself isn't selfish‚Äîit's necessary

Day 1: Just Survive

Essential Information

  • Your baby's nurse is your primary resource‚Äîask them anything
  • Write everything down‚Äîyou won't remember details later
  • Take photos of equipment settings and your baby's space for reference
  • It's okay to leave when you need to rest or eat

Basic NICU Orientation

  • Handwashing: The 3-minute scrub is required on first entry; regular washing thereafter
  • Visiting: Most NICUs allow 24/7 parent access (confirm your unit's policy)
  • Phone updates: You can call anytime‚Äîhave your baby's medical record number ready
  • Rounds: Usually occur between 8-11am; parents are typically welcome

You Have Permission To:

  • Feel overwhelmed, numb, or nothing at all
  • Go home and sleep in your own bed
  • Eat regular meals
  • Cry in the parking lot (or anywhere else)
  • Not understand everything right away
  • Ask the same question multiple times

Understanding Medical Terms & Equipment

Common Terms You'll Hear

Brady (Bradycardia) Heart rate drops below normal range - usually self-resolves
Desat (Desaturation) Oxygen levels drop temporarily - very common in preemies
Apnea Pause in breathing for 20+ seconds - monitors will alert immediately
Residuals Leftover milk in stomach at next feeding - helps assess feeding tolerance
NPO Nothing by mouth - baby temporarily can't have feeds
CPAP/BIPAP Breathing support that helps keep lungs open - not the same as a ventilator

Equipment Basics

Monitors & Alarms

‚ñº
  • Yellow alarms: Usually minor - staff aware but not urgent
  • Red alarms: Need attention - nurse will respond quickly
  • Frequent alarms: Normal in NICU - your baby is being closely watched
  • Heart rate (HR): Usually 120-160 for babies
  • Oxygen saturation (SpO2): Target varies by baby's condition
  • Respiratory rate (RR): Usually 30-60 breaths per minute

Breathing Support

‚ñº
  • Room air: No oxygen support needed
  • Nasal cannula: Small amount of oxygen through nose tubes
  • CPAP: Continuous pressure to keep lungs open
  • Ventilator: Machine breathes for baby when needed

Feeding Equipment

‚ñº
  • NG/OG tube: Feeding tube through nose or mouth to stomach
  • TPN/Lipids: IV nutrition when baby can't digest milk yet
  • Gavage feeding: Tube feeding (same as NG/OG feeding)

Daily NICU Life & Participation

Your Role in Baby's Care

  • Diaper changes: Most parents can start helping immediately
  • Temperature checks: Learn to help with routine cares
  • Mouth care: Comfort for babies on breathing support
  • Holding: When medically stable (ask your nurse)
  • Feeding: Bottle or breast when baby is ready
  • Bathing: Usually weekly, great bonding opportunity

Pumping & Feeding

Pumping Reality Check

  • Every 2-3 hours is ideal, but do what you can manage
  • Supply drops are normal during stress
  • Any amount helps‚Äîdon't compare to others
  • Hospital-grade pumps make a difference
  • Insurance often covers pump rental

What You Can Bring

  • Clothes: Once baby is stable (ask first)
  • Blankets: Small receiving blankets from home
  • Pictures: Family photos for the incubator
  • Music: Quiet music or recordings of your voice
  • Comfort items: Small stuffed animals (check with nurse)

Sample Daily Routine

8:00 AM Rounds - great time to hear the plan
9:00 AM Hands-on care time (diaper, temp, position)
12:00 PM Another care time - possible holding
3:00 PM Care time - practice feeding if ready
6:00 PM Evening cares - bath day?
9:00 PM Night cares - quiet bonding time

Emotional Support & Coping

This Is Trauma

Having a baby in the NICU is a traumatic experience. Your feelings‚Äîwhatever they are‚Äîare valid and normal. You don't need to be grateful, positive, or strong all the time.

Common Feelings (All Normal)

It's Normal To Feel:

  • Disconnected from your baby
  • Jealous of parents with healthy babies
  • Angry at your body
  • Guilty about everything and nothing
  • Numb or emotionally flat
  • Terrified one moment, hopeful the next
  • Resentful of the NICU routine
  • Like a visitor instead of a parent

Coping Strategies That Actually Help

  • Take breaks: Leaving doesn't make you a bad parent
  • Limit updates: Designate one person to update others
  • Set boundaries: It's okay to say no to visitors
  • Find YOUR routine: Don't compare to other NICU parents
  • Document when ready: Photos/journals can help process
  • Seek support: NICU PTSD is real‚Äîtherapy helps

Partner Dynamics

  • You might cope completely differently‚Äîthat's normal
  • Take turns being the "strong one"
  • Communicate needs directly‚Äîmind reading doesn't work in crisis
  • Divide responsibilities based on strengths
  • Respect each other's processing styles

When to Seek Immediate Support

  • Thoughts of harming yourself or others
  • Unable to eat or sleep for multiple days
  • Panic attacks or severe anxiety
  • Feeling disconnected from reality
  • Substance use to cope

Frequently Asked Questions

Why can't I hold my baby yet?

‚ñº

Babies need to be medically stable for holding. This usually means stable breathing, heart rate, and temperature. Every baby is different‚Äîsome can be held right away, others need more time. Your nurse will let you know when it's safe.

Why do different nurses do things differently?

‚ñº

While medical care is standardized, comfort techniques vary. Each nurse has their own successful methods for calming, positioning, and caring for babies. As long as medical protocols are followed, these variations are normal and actually beneficial‚Äîyour baby gets exposed to different effective techniques.

Is it my fault my baby is here?

‚ñº

No. Full stop. Prematurity and NICU admissions happen for countless reasons, most completely out of your control. Even if you had pregnancy complications, that doesn't mean you caused them. Your baby is getting the care they need, and that's what matters now.

When will my baby come home?

‚ñº

The "due date guideline" is just that‚Äîa guideline. Discharge depends on: feeding all bottles/breast, maintaining temperature, breathing without support, and gaining weight consistently. Some babies go home before their due date, others after. Your baby will show you when they're ready.

Preparing for Discharge

Signs You're Getting Close

  • Baby is taking all feeds by mouth
  • Maintaining temperature in an open crib
  • No breathing support needed
  • Steady weight gain
  • No bradys/desats for several days

Discharge Checklist

Medical Tasks
  • Car seat test completed
  • Hearing screen passed
  • Eye exam (if needed)
  • Medications understood
  • Follow-up appointments scheduled
Parent Education
  • CPR certification
  • Safe sleep practices
  • Feeding plan clear
  • Equipment training (if needed)
  • Emergency plan reviewed

Questions to Ask Before Leaving

  • What's our feeding schedule and volume?
  • When do we call the doctor vs emergency room?
  • What medications and when?
  • When is our first appointment?
  • Any restrictions (visitors, going out)?
  • Signs to watch for at home?

The Weird Feelings About Leaving

It's normal to feel terrified about discharge. You're leaving your safety net. Many parents feel guilty about "abandoning" their NICU nurses or sad about leaving this intense chapter behind. These mixed emotions are completely normal.

After NICU: The Fourth Trimester (Adjusted)

  • Use adjusted age for milestones until age 2
  • Extra vigilant about germs is appropriate
  • Sleep anxiety is common‚Äîconsider a monitor for peace of mind
  • NICU follow-up clinic will track development
  • Early intervention services may be recommended
  • NICU PTSD can emerge after discharge‚Äîget support
